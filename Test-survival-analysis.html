Interpretation of the graphical results

Non-epileptic patients seem to need more time to reach their final diagnosis. This finding can be explained by the fact that patients arriving to the ED for a suspition of first epileptic seizure were investigated for epilepsy and exclude this diagnosis took time. Regarding the relapses, the delay until the final diagnosis seems to be crucial for non-epileptic patients while the effect is much weaker for epileptic patients. However, that is not a conclusive proof that a swift intervention is beneficial. The effect can be carried by difficult cases which take more time to be diagnosed. Indeed, at 0 day, arround 40% of patients who will relapse don’t have their final diagnosis, compared to 20% for seizure-free patients.To overcome this problem, we might want to look at the delay between the first seizure and the first consultation by an epileptologist. Results show that epileptic patients tend to be seen in consultation by an epileptologist later than a non-epileptic patients (in median 8.5 days). The only way I see to explain that is that epileptic patients are often seen in the ED, the diagnosis and treatment are settled, which means that it is less urgent to see them compared to a non-epileptic patient for whom the diagnosis is still uncertain. Regarding relapses, the delay between the first event and the first consultation does not seem to impact relapses in epileptic patients. It might be possible that this delay was not relevant since number of patients are already treated in the ED, making the delay until the appointement with an epileptologist less relevant. Maybe the key variable is the delay between the first seizure and the day an AED was initiated. Results show that there is no impact of this delay. An explanation might be that doctors in the ED target correctly the patients who need to be treated immediately and those who don’t, cancelling the effect of delay. Patients with high risk of relapse are treated right away, while patients with a low relapse risk are treated later. Unfortunately, this hypothesis cannot be tested, since it would involve to randomly treat patients in different delays, which is not very ethical… In conclusion, there is probably no way to answer this question with these data.

data importation and transformation

delay was calculated, missing values were excluded, the unknown diagnosis were classified as censored, diagnoses were dichotomize in epilepsy, not epilepsy, only the positive delays were taken into account, relapses were dichotomize in relapses or no relapse. For epileptic patients only relapses under treatment were considered as relapses. Data were transformed in factor and in numerical data. Only delays under 2 years (730 days) were used.

Delay to the final diagnosis between epileptic and non-epileptic patients

This plot looks at the differences in delay to reach the final diagnosis between epileptic and non-epileptic patients.

Graphically, it seems that non-epileptic patients tend to reach their diagnosis later than epileptic patients –> Probably because the patients comes to the ER, where a first seizure is suspected and it takes time to rule out epilepsy. Another explanation could be that since the epilepsy-like event are less life threatning, it is less urgent to investigate (often syncopes).

Delay to the final diagnosis between epileptic and non-epileptic patients and adding the relapses information

Same model as before but adding the relapses information.

By adding the relapse information, it appears that the patients who relapse tend to recieve their diagnosis later than those who don’t. The effect mignt be explained by the difficulty to diagnose some patients. Patients who are hard to diagnose have more relapses until we are sure about the diagnosis and start a treatment. To see the real effect of the delay, it might be more appropriate to use another variable, such as the delay until the first consultation, in order to avoid the effect of difficulty to diagnose patients.

Delay between the first seizure and the first consultation by an epileptologist, between epileptic and non-epileptic patients

It seems that non-epileptic patients are seen in priority compared to epileptic patients. this counter intuitive result might be explained by the fact that easy epileptic cases are usually seen and treated in the ED, implying that the follow-up is not so urgent. However, for non-epileptic patients, there still might be a doubt on the diagnosis.
